The Beginning

If you are studying any kind of engineering at the University of Toronto, you will take a statics course during your first semester: Either CIV100 (Core 8) or CIV102 (EngSci). This page is meant to contain parts of all structural courses up to at least second-year Civil Engineering and may be expanded in the future as I see fit.

To ensure this page remains useful for all, I will specify which courses cover each concept I cover. They are in chronological order for CIV100 and other courses Civil Engineering students take. I do not know the EngSci order that well. Before doing statics, you should be familiar with dynamics concepts taught in high school, especially forces and spring motion. Statics courses assume a good understanding of the dynamics content in SPH3U and SPH4U. Some concepts in later sections will also require calculus knowledge (though not in CIV100).

Statics starts with a simple idea: A stable structure must be in equilibrium, i.e. \(\Sigma F = 0\). Since force is a vector, this must be true in all directions. In both CIV100 and CIV102, we look at this in 2D. In CIV100, we look at it in 3D as well (I do not believe CIV102 looks at it in 3D). Since we are attempting to find how a resultant force is equal to 0, it is important to know how to find a resultant force.

Vectors & Resultant forces (High School Review for CIV100 & CIV102)

If you are comfortable with vector addition, you can skip to the next section.

There are two methods of vector addition: law of cosines and using components. Law of cosines treats the two vectors, positioned head to tail, and the resultant as three edges of a triangle as shown below. If you have the magnitudes of each vector and the angle \(\theta\), you can use cosine law to find the magnitude of the resultant. 1. If \(\vec v_1\) and \(\vec v_2 \) are attached tail-to-tail with an angle of \(30\) degrees and magnitudes of \(3\) and \(5\), respectively, what is the magnitude of \(\vec v_1 + \vec v_2\)?
